01 · The problem

Europe's youngest citizens have their data running through Big Tech.

Across the continent, kindergartens run on WhatsApp groups and generic apps never built for them. Photos, health notes, allergies, where a child is at any hour: their entire day flows through platforms outside European jurisdiction, built to monetize data rather than protect it.

🛰️

Data outside EU jurisdiction

Sensitive records on children pass through US-owned platforms that can be compelled to disclose under foreign law.

🔒

No sovereign alternative

There is no dedicated, EU-hosted platform for childcare communication, so institutions default to whatever Big Tech offers.

🤝

A weakened public sphere

The most foundational relationship, family and educator, is mediated by infrastructure no European authority controls.

02 · A European precedent

Denmark already showed both the demand and the sovereignty gap.

1

Denmark built AULA

A dedicated national parent-educator platform. One channel for the whole relationship. No WhatsApp. Families embraced it as the way schools and homes communicate.

2

But AULA runs on AWS

Even this flagship public platform sits on US-owned cloud infrastructure, and that became the sovereignty problem.

3

The regulator refused

Denmark's own data authority, Datatilsynet, declined to approve the arrangement over fears US authorities could compel access to children's data.

Amazon's guarantees that children's data is safe from US authorities were "pure theater."

David Heinemeier Hansson (creator of Ruby on Rails), on the AULA / AWS arrangement.
Reported by radar.dk, 2023.

A whole nation wanted this. A genuinely sovereign version still doesn't exist.

The market treats EU data residency as optional. KinderGuard treats it as the product. Children's data lives on European infrastructure. Never AWS, Azure or GCP.

Data protection
